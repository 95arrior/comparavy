# Comparavy Writing Standard

Comparavy is a US-focused, AdSense-first AI problem-solving content site. A successful Comparavy guide helps a reader finish a practical job with AI, then decide which tools are worth trying. The article should feel useful before the reader scrolls.

## 1. Core Editorial Mission

Comparavy helps people save time, study better, create content faster, run small business tasks, improve freelance workflows, and use AI in practical, realistic ways. Every guide must solve a real reader problem. Tools are supporting evidence, not the article's reason to exist.

The reader should leave with:

- A direct answer in the first 100 words.
- A realistic workflow they can try today.
- A clear sense of which tool to start with and why.
- A reason to trust the limitations, warnings, and next step.

## Topic and SEO Research Gate

Before writing, approving, or publishing any AteFlo shortcut, validate the
topic with live search-intent research. Use the full gate in
`docs/editorial/TOPIC_SEO_RESEARCH_GATE.md`.

At minimum, the topic must prove:

- It matches a real task-based search query, preferably a long-tail query.
- The live Google SERP leaves room for a practical prompt-builder page.
- Google Trends, Trending Now, Keyword Planner, Search Console, or owner data
  support the timing when that data is relevant or available.
- The shortcut has a clear input, finished output, prompt workflow, example
  output, and review checklist.
- It is not merely a broad AI tools article or a repeat of an existing
  published shortcut.

Hold or reject topics that cannot pass this gate.

## Prompt Builder Usability Test

Every new AteFlo shortcut must be simple enough for a reader who has never written an AI prompt before.

Before a guide can be approved, check:

- Does the public page use one primary generated prompt instead of multiple prompt choices?
- Does the builder explain that filling in details improves the result but is not required?
- Are optional fields hidden behind a simple "More details" toggle when useful?
- Does it show what the user will make?
- Does the prompt work in ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot, or another AI chat tool?
- Does the prompt avoid internal AteFlo-only language?
- Does the prompt include clear placeholders when fields are blank?
- Does the prompt include safety rules when missing facts, private details, claims, deadlines, or promises matter?
- Can a beginner copy it without editing much?
- Can an older or non-technical user understand the flow?
- Is review guidance presented as a simple checklist unless the topic truly needs another prompt?

AI editorial review should score each guide on Copy-Paste Readiness, Beginner Simplicity, Prompt Builder Usability, Finished Output Clarity, and Safety/Trust. The publish threshold remains 85.

## 2. US Audience Writing Style

Write in direct US English. Use plain wording, short paragraphs, concrete examples, and practical tradeoffs. Avoid hype, vague productivity language, academic filler, and affiliate-style ranking language.

Prefer:

- "Start with the meeting notes and mark decisions, owners, dates, and open questions."
- "Use the tool for the first draft, then check every commitment before sending."

Avoid:

- "In today's digital world, AI can transform your productivity."
- "This strong option fits your situation."

## 3. AdSense-First Content Rules

The guide must be useful without a click or purchase. Do not make the article feel like an affiliate wrapper. Do not hide the answer behind tool cards. Do not recommend paid plans before the reader has tested a real task.

Required:

- Clear educational value.
- Original workflow guidance.
- Transparent uncertainty.
- No fake testing claims.
- No exact current pricing unless verified by a maintained source.
- No pressure to subscribe.

## 4. Mobile-First Readability Rules

Most readers scan first. Make the first screen useful.

- Start with a direct answer.
- Use short paragraphs.
- Put the workflow before long tool cards in how-to guides.
- Keep lists scannable.
- Avoid intros that explain AI in general.
- Make mobile workflow advice specific to quick review, approvals, drafts, checks, or small edits.

## 5. Desktop-Depth Rules

Desktop readers often want comparison depth, files, side-by-side review, and a more deliberate workflow.

Desktop guidance should explain:

- What is easier on a computer.
- What should be compared side by side.
- Which source material should stay visible.
- What should be reviewed before sending, publishing, studying from, or saving.

## 6. Strong First 100 Words

The first 100 words must satisfy Instant Reward Psychology: the reader gets a useful answer immediately and feels the article matches the search.

Must include:

- The specific job the reader wants done.
- The likely starting input.
- The desired output.
- The first practical action.
- A warning about review when accuracy matters.

Must not include:

- Generic AI background.
- Delayed answer.
- Tool ranking before the solution.
- "AI can help..." filler.

## 7. Quick Answer / Quick Verdict

Quick Answer is for how-to guides. It must tell the reader how to solve the workflow.

Quick Verdict is for tool-decision guides. It must tell the reader which tool to start with, when to pick the second option, and when to avoid the top pick.

Weak:

- "These tools can help you save time and improve your workflow."

Strong:

- "Start by pasting the cleaned meeting notes into Otter or Fireflies, ask for decisions, owners, dates, and open questions, then rewrite the result as a client email only after you verify every commitment."

## 8. Real Workflows

A workflow is not a feature list. It should show the reader what to do, why it matters, and what output they should have after each step.

Each workflow step should include:

- Input.
- Action.
- Review point.
- Output.
- Tool role only when the tool actually helps that step.

## 9. Tool Recommendations Without Becoming a Tool List

Tool recommendations must support the user problem.

Good tool guidance explains:

- What the tool does in this workflow.
- What input it handles best.
- What result it should produce.
- When another tool is better.
- What to check before trusting the output.

Reject repeated "Best for" or "Avoid if" phrasing. Each tool card must make a distinct practical claim.

## 10. High-Intent FAQ

FAQs should answer questions a real searcher would ask while trying to finish the job.

Good FAQs:

- Mention the task, source material, output, or tool choice.
- Resolve a buying, workflow, safety, or device question.
- Help the reader avoid a mistake.

Weak FAQs:

- "What is AI?"
- "How does AI work?"
- "Which AI tool is best?"

## 11. Avoid Generic AI Filler

Reject phrases that could fit any article:

- selected for this guide
- core workflow
- fits your situation
- strong option
- useful for this use case
- in today's digital world
- leverage AI
- unlock productivity
- rough content that needs rewriting or repackaging
- final cleanup
- different workflow

## 12. Income-Intent Content

Income guides must be realistic and careful. No guaranteed income. No get-rich-quick language. No implied client demand without evidence.

Must explain:

- Skill needed.
- Realistic service offer.
- Time and effort.
- Cost and tools.
- First small step.
- Limitations and risks.

## 13. Fake Testing and Fake Certainty

Do not claim Comparavy tested a tool unless there is real maintained test evidence in the repo. Do not claim exact prices, current launches, legal compliance, guaranteed accuracy, or guaranteed earnings.

Use careful language:

- "Check the official site before subscribing."
- "Use this as a draft, then verify names, dates, numbers, and claims."

## 14. Final Verdicts

The final verdict should tell the reader what to do next. It should not repeat the intro.

Good final verdicts:

- Name the best first action.
- Explain when to choose the top tool.
- Explain when to use an alternative.
- Point uncertain readers to /finder.

## 15. Mandatory Rejection Rules

Reject or rewrite any guide that:

- Does not answer the problem in the first 100 words.
- Does not solve a clear user problem.
- Starts with generic AI background.
- Sends readers to tool cards before explaining the solution.
- Uses banned generic phrases.
- Has repeated Best for / Avoid if text.
- Has generic FAQ.
- Has shallow mobile or desktop sections.
- Drifts into the wrong topic bucket.
- Invents testing, pricing, current news, income, or certainty.
- Has no concrete workflow where one is required.
- Has no example result where one is required.
